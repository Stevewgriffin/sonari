## APPETITE DRIVER — 18 Questions

### D1.1: Stimulation-Seeking (4)
- A1: I feel most alive when experiencing something new, challenging, or intellectually stimulating.
- A2: I tend to pursue new interests and initiatives with enthusiasm but sometimes move on before full completion.
- A3: I'm drawn to experiences and challenges that push my thinking or boundaries in meaningful ways.
- A4: When life or work becomes too routine or predictable, I feel a restlessness.

### D1.2: Hedonic/Comfort-Seeking (4)
- A5: I find satisfaction in familiar routines, established traditions, and things I know well.
- A6: When faced with stress or pressure, I reach for food, entertainment, or other comforts.
- A7: I sometimes indulge more than I intend (eating, leisure, spending) even when I know I should moderate.
- A8: I'm intentional and disciplined about how I allocate resources to comfort and leisure. [REVERSE]

### D1.3: Gratification Timing (2)
- A9: When something genuinely excites or engages me, I commit to it with full energy.
- A10: I'm more energized by periods of intensity and challenge than by sustained, even-paced work.

### D1.4: Boredom Relationship (2)
- A12: Monotony and boredom are genuinely difficult states for me to tolerate.
- A13: Extended periods of routine or predictability drain my energy over time.

### D1.5: Sensory/Aesthetic (1)
- A15: I notice and appreciate beauty, aesthetics, and sensory richness more than most people.

### D1.6: Risk Tolerance (1)
- A18: I'm drawn toward pursuits — professional or personal — that carry some element of risk or uncertainty.

### D1.7: Humor/Levity (4)
- A21: I see humor in life's challenges and use levity to navigate difficulty.
- A22: I can laugh at hard things without diminishing their reality or making others feel dismissed.
- A23: People would describe me as naturally lighthearted and someone who brings levity to situations.
- A24: Humor is one of my core tools for processing difficult things without avoiding them.

## APPROVAL DRIVER — 18 Questions

### D2.1: Public/Social Validation (4)
- B1: I'm aware of how I'm perceived in my professional and social circles and it influences how I present myself.
- B2: I feel a genuine lift when I receive recognition from respected colleagues, peers, or community members.
- B3: When I accomplish something significant, I'm aware of wanting people in my circles to know about it.
- B4: My sense of how I'm doing is affected by how I'm perceived and valued by people I respect.

### D2.2: Peer/Professional Validation (2)
- B5: I appreciate acknowledgment from colleagues when I've made a meaningful contribution.
- B6: When my work or leadership goes unrecognized by people I respect, it bothers me.

### D2.3: Relational/Intimate Validation (2)
- B8: When someone close to me seems disappointed or distant, I tend to replay interactions looking for what I missed.
- B11: I sometimes say yes to people I care about even when it would be more honest to say no.

### D2.4: Comparison Orientation (2)
- B12: I'm aware of how my accomplishments and standing compare to peers at my life stage.
- B13: When someone in my circles achieves something significant, I notice my internal response before genuine happiness.

### D2.5: Conflict Avoidance vs. Relational Courage (2)
- B15: I tell people what I honestly see and believe, even if it might create tension.
- B16: I speak up in professional or personal settings even when it risks friction with people who matter.

### D2.6: Belonging/Community (2)
- B18: I feel genuinely energized by being part of meaningful teams, organizations, or communities.
- B19: I invest in the health and cohesion of groups I'm part of, even at personal cost.

### D2.7: Rejection Sensitivity (2)
- B21: When I feel rejected or excluded by someone important, it affects my mood and sense of wellbeing.
- B22: When I sense someone I value pulling away, I tend to work to understand and re-establish connection.

## AMBITION DRIVER — 26 Questions

### D3.1: Achievement Orientation (3)
- C1: I feel a pull toward meaningful productivity and progress even during periods meant for rest.
- C2: I take satisfaction in completing significant work and achieving important milestones.
- C3: When I'm not making progress toward something that matters, I feel dissatisfied.

### D3.2: Impact/Legacy (4)
- C4: I think regularly about the lasting impact and legacy of my work and contributions.
- C5: I'm motivated to invest in people and initiatives whose significance will outlast my direct involvement.
- C6: I measure my life's value by lasting impact and influence, not just immediate results.
- C7: I'm drawn to work and causes that matter at a scope beyond my immediate organization or community.

### D3.3: Recognition/Status — Scenarios (4)
- **C8:** You led a significant initiative to success. The organization celebrates the team's achievement, but your specific leadership isn't highlighted. Your honest feeling:
  - a. The initiative succeeded and the team was recognized — that's what matters most.
  - b. Mild wistfulness about not being acknowledged, but it passes.
  - c. Internal frustration about the credit distribution that I manage internally.
  - d. Frustration that meaningfully affects how I experience the achievement.

- **C9:** You invested substantially in developing and mentoring someone. They go on to significant success without acknowledging your role. Your response:
  - a. Genuine satisfaction — that was exactly what the investment was for.
  - b. Mild disappointment that passes relatively quickly.
  - c. A sense of loss about the unacknowledged contribution that I carry.
  - d. Real hurt — it feels like an oversight of what mattered.

- **C10:** You led a significant initiative that fails publicly. You were clearly responsible for key decisions. In the weeks after:
  - a. I own it fully and directly, then focus on lessons and moving forward.
  - b. I own it publicly but privately revisit what others could have handled differently.
  - c. I take ownership but find the public nature of the failure genuinely difficult.
  - d. I struggle to separate my assessment of the failure from my assessment of my own capability.

- **C11:** Someone you believe is less qualified is placed in a leadership role above you. You're convinced it's the wrong choice. Over the following months:
  - a. I commit to making them successful — the organization's success is what matters.
  - b. I perform my role well while privately holding my assessment of the decision.
  - c. My engagement is genuinely affected, though I manage my behavior professionally.
  - d. I look seriously for other opportunities — I can't sustain full contribution in this structure.

### D3.4: Competitive Orientation (3)
- C12: I'm aware of how my accomplishments and standing compare to accomplished peers.
- C13: The achievements of people I respect in my field motivate me to maintain or raise my own performance.
- C14: I'm energized by environments where excellence is recognized and accomplishments are acknowledged.

### D3.5: Leadership/Influence (3)
- C15: I feel most effective and most like myself when I'm shaping strategy and direction.
- C16: I'm drawn to roles where significant outcomes depend on my ability to lead and influence people.
- C17: It's difficult for me to fully engage without being in a position to influence direction and vision.

### D3.6: Mastery/Excellence (3)
- C18: I find it hard to let work go that I know could be more refined, even when further improvement won't be visible.
- C19: I'd rather develop deep expertise and mastery in key areas than be broadly competent across many.
- C20: I hold myself and my contributions to standards that most would consider quite rigorous.
